Mean, Spread, and the Extremes

A small centre shift can become a large tail-ratio shift. The key move is to read group differences in standard-deviation units, not as percentages of the mean.

The central question is how two normally distributed populations can look similar near the centre yet differ sharply in the tails. Mean differences become mathematically interpretable when they are related to the standard deviations of the distributions.

The mathematical core

The centre matters, but the centre alone is not the story. What matters for extremes is where the cutoff sits in each distribution’s own standard-deviation units.

1. Same spread, different mean

Start with one shared cutoff: \(c=\mu_A+k\sigma_A\). For group A, that cutoff is exactly \(k\) SDs above its mean. If group B's mean is \(d_A\) SDs higher, the same cutoff is closer to B's centre. In B's own units it is \((k-d_A)/r\) SDs above B's mean, where \(r=\sigma_B/\sigma_A\). With equal SDs, \(r=1\), so \(d_A=0.5\) makes A's \(k=3\) cutoff become only \(2.5\) SDs above B's mean. That is why the upper-tail ratio is about \(4.6{:}1\), even though the two curves still overlap heavily near the centre.

In the atlas, \(c\) is chosen by a neutral rule: \(c_+=(\mu_M+\mu_W)/2+2\sigma_{\mathrm{pooled}}\). That means the cutoff is two pooled SDs above the midpoint between the two means. It is not necessarily a score whose male and female z-scores average to exactly \(2\); that only works cleanly when the two SDs are equal.

2. Same mean, different spread

A group with larger SD can be overrepresented in both tails, even with no mean difference. Unequal variance can therefore change the extremes without changing the average.

3. Mean and spread together

When a mean shift and an SD shift point in the same tail direction, the tail ratio can grow quickly. When they point in opposite directions, one can offset the other.

Univariate normal comparison
\[ A \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_A,\sigma_A^2) \] \[ B \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_B,\sigma_B^2) \] \[ d_A=\frac{\mu_B-\mu_A}{\sigma_A} \] \[ r=\frac{\sigma_B}{\sigma_A} \]
Upper tail
\[ c_+=\mu_A+k\sigma_A \] \[ p_{A,+}=1-\Phi(k) \] \[ p_{B,+}=1-\Phi\!\left(\frac{k-d_A}{r}\right) \] \[ R_+=\frac{p_{B,+}}{p_{A,+}} \]
Lower tail
\[ c_-=\mu_A-k\sigma_A \] \[ p_{A,-}=\Phi(-k) \] \[ p_{B,-}=\Phi\!\left(\frac{-k-d_A}{r}\right) \] \[ R_-=\frac{p_{B,-}}{p_{A,-}} \]
Tail-ratio function
\[ R_+(k;d_A,r)= \frac{1-\Phi\!\left((k-d_A)/r\right)}{1-\Phi(k)} \]

Mean shift

With equal SDs, a positive mean shift reduces the effective upper-tail cutoff from \(k\) to \(k-d_A\). The farther into the tail you go, the more that small subtraction matters.

SD ratio

If \(r>1\), group B has thicker modeled tails relative to group A. That can increase both upper and lower extremes even when \(d_A=0\).

Composition

With base rates \(\pi_A\) and \(\pi_B\), B's share above a cutoff is \(\pi_Bp_{B,+}/(\pi_Ap_{A,+}+\pi_Bp_{B,+})\). Equal base rates reduce this to \(p_{B,+}/(p_{A,+}+p_{B,+})\).

Extreme growth

For equal SDs and large \(k\), Mills' approximation gives \(R_+\approx\frac{k}{k-d_A}\exp(kd_A-d_A^2/2)\). The product \(kd_A\) is why the same mean shift has a larger effect farther into the tail.

Methods and limits

The main intellectual risk is to overread clean curves. The curves clarify a mathematical relationship; they do not turn every dataset into a true Gaussian population.

Normal approximation

For continuous measurements, a normal curve can be a useful compact summary. For ordinal scales, binary variables, top-tail sport samples, and skewed behavior counts, it is a visualization model rather than a distributional claim.

Tail ratios are threshold-dependent

Tail ratios can look modest near the centre and very large at extreme cutoffs. Reporting the cutoff is part of the claim.

Tail composition is not individual prediction

Large overlap means many individuals sit against the group-level pattern. Tail ratios describe aggregate composition above or below a threshold, not a verdict about a person.
